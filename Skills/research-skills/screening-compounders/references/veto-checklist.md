# Veto Checklist

Apply these tests before any scoring. One confirmed trigger is enough to eliminate a company.

## 1. Management or control-person integrity failure

Fail if there is credible evidence of:
- fraud, accounting manipulation, bribery, repeated misleading disclosure, or serious governance abuse
- repeated related-party transactions that disadvantage outside shareholders
- recent governance deterioration such as board weakening, unusual voting control changes, or compensation abuse

Evidence sources:
- SEC enforcement actions, court records, proxy statements, company filings, high-quality investigative reporting

## 2. Distressed-turnaround story

Fail if the thesis depends mainly on:
- a celebrity CEO repairing a broken company
- a "new era" narrative after prolonged operational decline
- recovery assumptions rather than proven long-duration excellence

## 3. High leverage

Fail if:
- `Net Debt / EBITDA > 2`, or
- interest-bearing debt is clearly heavy relative to cash generation, or
- the balance sheet became meaningfully weaker after recent capital allocation

Use the latest annual filing and, if needed, the latest quarter to confirm debt, cash, and EBITDA.

## 4. Acquisition addiction

Fail if the company repeatedly relies on sizable acquisitions to maintain growth, enter categories, or mask weakness.

Red flags:
- multiple large acquisitions within the last `5` years
- serial roll-up behavior
- goodwill / intangible build-up disconnected from organic economics
- major destructive deal history

## 5. Fast-changing industry with low predictability of winners

Fail if the industry structure makes long-term winner prediction inherently weak, especially when:
- product cycles are short
- switching costs are low
- advantages decay quickly
- disruption risk dominates historical evidence

This veto should be applied carefully. A fast-growing market is not automatically a fail; the question is whether durable winner identification is realistically possible.

## 6. Misaligned owner economics

Fail if outside shareholders are structurally disadvantaged, including:
- government-controlled entities
- subsidiaries trapped inside complex groups
- opaque conglomerate structures
- cross-holdings or control arrangements that clearly dilute minority-owner alignment

## Additional automatic fails

Reject if recent evidence shows any of the following:
- governance deterioration
- destructive capital allocation, especially value-destructive acquisitions
- moat impairment that appears structurally irreversible

## Documentation Standard

For every company, record:
- `Pass / Fail`
- triggered item number(s)
- the exact evidence
- source link or filing citation
- brief explanation of why it is disqualifying
