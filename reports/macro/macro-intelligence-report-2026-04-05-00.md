## Detailed Summary

The most important new fact remains the March U.S. employment report released on April 3. The Bureau of Labor Statistics said nonfarm payrolls increased by 178,000 in March, unemployment was 4.3%, and average hourly earnings rose 0.2% month over month and 3.5% year over year. That is not a recession print. It is a resilience print with only moderate wage pressure. Source: [BLS Employment Situation](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.htm).

The market message from that report was not panic, but it was not relief either. Because U.S. equities were shut for Good Friday, the cleanest immediate reaction came through futures, rates, the dollar, and gold. Second, the oil/geopolitical backdrop is hostile enough that even stable growth can feel inflationary again. AP's Friday report pointed back to Thursday's oil surge, with U.S. crude at $111.54 and Brent at $109.03 as fears around disruption to Strait of Hormuz traffic persisted. This is a world where growth is slowing unevenly and disinflation is no longer falling cleanly into central banks' laps.
