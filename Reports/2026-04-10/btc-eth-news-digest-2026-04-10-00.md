That is not bad for ETH, but it is not leadership. The desk-level read is that institutions are willing to re-engage with the cleaner macro asset first, while still treating ETH as a higher-beta follower. Sources: [Farside BTC ETF flows](https://farside.co.uk/bitcoin-etf-flow-all-data/), [Farside ETH ETF flows](https://farside.co.uk/ethereum-etf-flow-all-data/).

Macro is still the controlling variable. Reuters-based FX coverage said the dollar index slipped to 98.82 on April 9 as ceasefire hopes supported risk assets, while Reuters energy coverage said Brent still settled at $99.88 and WTI at $97.22. That mix matters because softer DXY helps both BTC and ETH, but oil still near $100 keeps CPI risk alive into Friday's U.S. inflation release. Sources: [Reuters on the dollar](https://www.xm.com/research/markets/allNews/reuters/us-dollar-slips-as-stable-iran-ceasefire-hopes-spur-flight-to-risk-assets-55144093), [Reuters on oil](https://www.rigzone.com/news/wire/oil_settles_up_1_on_saudi_iran_conflict_hormuz_risks-09-apr-2026-180598-article/), [BLS release schedule](https://www.bls.gov/schedule/2026/04_sched_list.htm).

My judgment is that BTC has the cleaner 1 to 3 session setup, while ETH needs a friendlier macro tape to outperform materially. If CPI is benign and DXY keeps softening, BTC can probe higher and ETH can follow with beta. If CPI is sticky, BTC likely holds up better because the ETF bid and safer-macro framing are stronger there than in ETH.
