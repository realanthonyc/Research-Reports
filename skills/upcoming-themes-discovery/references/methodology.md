# Methodology

Use this reference when deciding whether a theme is early, durable, and investable rather than merely topical.

## Core idea

Search for `future demand regimes`, not "what is hot now."
The target is a theme that could become one of the next major market focuses later, while still being early enough today that it has not fully moved into consensus ownership or narrative saturation.

A future demand regime should satisfy most of the following:

1. Demand can plausibly accelerate within `6-24 months`
2. Demand can plausibly persist for `1-2 years` or longer
3. Demand can become large enough to matter for industry earnings and market attention
4. Value capture can be traced to identifiable parts of the value chain
5. Future supply response is limited enough to preserve attractive economics
6. Market expectations still appear incomplete
7. There are early hints of positioning or industry preparation, but not yet full-blown crowding

## Best intellectual mix

Use these traditions together rather than relying on one school alone:

- `Peter Lynch / Philip Fisher`: use observation plus field evidence to generate and verify candidate themes
- `Warren Buffett`: separate exciting demand from attractive business economics
- `Capital cycle`: ask whether future supply will destroy returns
- `Rogers / Moore / Christensen`: place the theme in the adoption curve
- `Helpman / Trajtenberg / Klepper`: identify the buildout, diffusion, and industry-life-cycle stage
- `Mauboussin`: ask whether the market has already priced in the likely upside

## The five mandatory tests

### 1. Structural driver test

Ask:

- What force is likely to make spending rise?
- Why is it structural rather than temporary?
- Why should it matter within the next `6-24 months`?

High-quality drivers include:

- regulation, mandates, or compliance deadlines
- government budgets and industrial policy
- enterprise or infrastructure capex
- falling cost curves
- labor scarcity and automation pressure
- resilience, security, reliability, or redundancy requirements
- bottlenecks in power, cooling, networking, logistics, water, or other physical systems

Also ask:

- can this move from "interesting growth pocket" to "must-fund spending category"?
- can this create enough revenue or capex to matter at sector scale?
- is this still early enough that the average investor has not fully framed it as the next obvious trade?

### 2. Adoption-stage test

Classify the theme:

- `concept only`
- `pre-inflection`
- `adoption inflection`
- `scaling`
- `mature / crowded`

Best hunting ground:

- real theme
- adoption likely within `6-18 months`
- evidence forming, but consensus still fragmented
- demand magnitude large enough that successful adoption would reshape budgets or capacity plans
- early specialist positioning or industry preparation may be visible, but broad market ownership is not yet extreme

Too early:

- technically promising
- weak procurement evidence
- unclear budgeting path
- still trapped in pilots or proofs of concept

Too late:

- media saturation is extreme
- valuation already assumes success
- second-order beneficiaries are fully recognized

### 3. Value-chain economics test

For each theme, break the chain into:

- enablers
- components
- integrators
- applications
- recurring or compliance layers

Then ask:

- where is scarcity?
- where is pricing power?
- where are switching costs?
- where is customer dependence lowest?
- where can returns on capital remain high?

Remember:

- the most visible company is often not the best equity
- the earliest revenue growth node is often not the highest-return node

### 4. Durability and capital-cycle test

Positive durability evidence:

- multi-year budget commitments
- backlog or bookings expansion
- deployment timetables
- standards or legal requirements
- long asset build cycles
- repeat spending rather than one-time purchases

Capital-cycle danger signs:

- low barriers to entry
- rapid capacity additions
- many copycat entrants
- commoditization risk
- customer bargaining power rising too fast

### 5. Mispricing test

Ask whether the market is still missing something important:

- consensus treats the theme as cyclical or niche
- valuation remains anchored to legacy mix
- current earnings understate future earning power
- the market focuses on flashy leaders and ignores scarce enablers
- near-term noise obscures medium-term demand
- some informed money or industry actors may already be leaning in, but broad consensus still underestimates the theme

If the theme is real but already fully understood and fully priced, it may still be interesting, but it is no longer the best fit for this skill.

## Creative edge

Many of the best future themes first appear as unavoidable spending categories rather than glamorous narratives.

Look for:

- compliance spending
- resilience or redundancy spending
- labor-replacement spending
- safety and reliability spending
- power, cooling, and networking spend behind digital themes
- infrastructure required by a larger narrative

This framing often produces better ambush candidates than scanning for obvious concept names.

## Smart-money and industry-positioning hints

Treat these as supportive signals:

- niche funds or specialist investors start discussing a theme before the broad sell side does
- management teams begin small but deliberate capacity additions
- suppliers report unusual inquiry growth before revenue fully inflects
- second-order beneficiaries begin strengthening before the headline names become crowded

Do not use these hints as standalone proof.
They matter only when they reinforce a structurally strong, durable, and underpriced demand thesis.

## Creative search prompts

When researching, actively search beyond the headline narrative. Good prompts include:

- what spending categories become impossible to defer if this trend continues?
- what hidden bottleneck will absorb the next dollar of capex?
- which "boring" suppliers become scarce when a glamorous theme scales?
- what has to be built, certified, cooled, powered, repaired, tested, financed, or insured for the headline theme to work?
- what adjacent market could become much larger because a different narrative is taking off?

This often surfaces better ideas than asking only "what sector benefits from X?"

## Failure modes to avoid

- confusing thematic excitement with budgeted demand
- ignoring supply response
- ignoring value-chain economics
- extrapolating too far from one press release or one flagship customer
- treating "good industry" as identical to "good stock"
- writing a long theme list without ranking conviction
