That relief move matters, but the broader macro message is still conflicted. The market is trying to price two different things at once: first, a possible easing of the geopolitical oil shock; second, the damage already done by March's energy surge to inflation expectations, growth confidence, and rate-cut hopes. Reuters reported gold was still headed for its worst month in more than 17 years even after rebounding on March 31, with spot gold around $4,578.89 and futures around $4,611.30 in early trade as the market reassessed whether the war would stay short enough to cap inflation damage. At the same time, Reuters analysis said Treasury investors were increasingly focused not just on oil, but on the fiscal and bond-market cost of a longer conflict. My judgment for the next several U.S. sessions is that this is a `growth-slowing, inflation-risky, liquidity-still-functional` regime. The new bullish evidence is that equities were able to stage a powerful relief rebound without an obvious policy pivot. The bearish evidence is that the rally was headline-led, March was still deeply negative for risk assets, euro area inflation jumped back above target on energy, and the next hard test is still ahead in U.S. data. BLS shows March payrolls are due on Friday, April 3 at 8:30 a.m. EDT, while ISM says March manufacturing PMI is due on Wednesday, April 1 at 10:00 a.m. ET. If the war premium fades and U.S. activity data softens, the market can extend the relief through lower yields and a softer dollar. If activity stays resilient while energy stays expensive, the market is back in the stagflation trap very quickly. ## US Macro Environment

The U.S. is still facing a policy-mix problem, not a clean recession problem. Energy has been the shock, but the market is increasingly sensitive to whether that shock stays temporary enough to avoid contaminating broader inflation and fiscal expectations. Reuters' Treasury analysis made that explicit: investors are now considering the budget and issuance cost of a prolonged conflict, not just the first-round oil move. Source: [Reuters Treasury analysis](https://mix929.com/2026/03/31/analysis-treasury-markets-next-test-rising-war-costs/).

The next directional pivot comes from activity data, not another headline recap. ISM on April 1 will tell the market whether prices paid are reheating faster than growth is cooling. Payrolls on April 3 will determine whether the bond market has enough labor-softening evidence to justify a more durable fall in yields. Source: [ISM](https://www.ismworld.org/supply-management-news-and-reports/reports/ism-pmi-reports/), [BLS](https://www.bls.gov/schedule/news_release/empsit.htm).

## Global Macro Environment

China modestly improved the global backdrop. Official PMI returning to 50.4 matters because it says China's factory sector is not collapsing into the energy shock; instead, it is still generating some support for industrial demand and regional risk sentiment. That is not enough to rescue the world on its own, but it helps prevent a synchronized growth scare. Sources: [AP](https://apnews.com/article/1dece7eda196b93706091b7b52eef94a), [Reuters](https://kelo.com/2026/03/30/chinas-factory-activity-returns-to-expansion-pmi-shows/).

Japan is the more nuanced global signal. Reuters Tankan improved in March, which confirms that semiconductors and manufacturing momentum were still helping, but the forward look weakened. That combination says Japan is not yet a liquidity accident, but it is vulnerable if external demand and energy costs both deteriorate. Source: [Trading Economics / Reuters Tankan](https://tradingeconomics.com/japan/reuters-tankan-index/news/534123).

Europe remains the cleanest warning signal. Euro area inflation back above 2% because of energy gives the ECB less room to lean dovish into a growth shock, which tightens the global policy mix rather than easing it. Source: [Reuters](https://mix929.com/2026/03/31/euro-zone-inflation-surges-past-ecb-target-on-oil-shock/).

## Interest Rates And Liquidity

The regime is still `liquidity-expanding` only in a narrow sense: the Fed is not actively breaking funding conditions, but it is not delivering a broad easing cycle either. The March 18 implementation note kept Treasury bill purchases and reserve maintenance in place, which matters because it means policy liquidity is not the immediate failure point. Source: [Federal Reserve implementation note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260318a1.htm).

Market liquidity, however, remains event-driven. March 31's equity rally looked like a relief squeeze more than a stable re-risking backed by clean macro data. That means the next move in yields still matters more than the day-one headline reaction. If payrolls soften, duration-sensitive assets can extend. If inflation-sensitive data stays hot, the bond market may struggle with both fiscal supply and sticky inflation risk. Source: [Reuters Treasury analysis](https://mix929.com/2026/03/31/analysis-treasury-markets-next-test-rising-war-costs/).

The clearest flow migration is out of panic defense and into tactical beta, but not into blind risk-chasing. March 31's strongest signal was the rebound in technology and broad equities once the market saw a chance of de-escalation. Yet gold's bounce and crypto's mixed reaction show that investors are not abandoning hedges outright.

My read is that stronger hands are rotating from outright crisis positioning into selective re-risking, not declaring the all-clear. That is consistent with a tape where institutions still want optionality into payrolls and into the next Iran headline rather than maximum exposure. Evidence remains mixed enough that I would call confidence on the smart-money read `moderate`, not high.

## Market Positioning And Sentiment

March 31's rally was large enough to force some short-covering and momentum re-risking, but monthly losses remained severe enough that positioning is still brittle. That combination creates a market where the next macro print can still move assets disproportionately.

The highest-conviction positioning conclusion is not that fear is gone. It is that fear became two-sided: investors no longer need maximum immediate defense if war de-escalation is credible, but they still need hedges because the macro aftershocks are unresolved.
